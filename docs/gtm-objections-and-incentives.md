# CropConnect — GTM: Objections & the Handshake Question

_A field guide for the conversation that happens when a rep is standing in the farmer's barn._
_Prepared June 2026. Strategy doc, not gospel — pressure-test against real visits._

4. "I already spend my evenings on paperwork. I don't want more screens."
5. "Just call me or text me. Why do I need software?"
6. "I'll look at it later." (= never)
7. "My internet out here is terrible."
8. "I don't want notifications buzzing me all day."
9. "Setting up my whole inventory sounds like a weekend of work."
10. "Can't you just do it for me?"

### B. Trust & skepticism (no proof yet)
11. "Never heard of you. Who's actually using this?"
12. "How do I know the buyer will pay?"
13. "What happens when a buyer stiffs me — do you cover it?"
14. "You're a startup. Will you even be here next season?"
15. "Some app took a cut and disappeared on a buddy of mine."
16. "Who sees my prices? I don't want competitors knowing."
17. "Is this just going to sell my data?"
18. "Sounds like every other 'farm-to-table' platform that folded."
19. "Show me a farmer like me who made money on this."
20. "Why are you giving me this for free? What's the catch?"

### C. Economic (the money math)
21. "What's it cost me? I run on 3% margins."
22. "If you're taking a fee, that comes straight out of my pocket."
23. "The courier fee makes my produce too expensive for them to buy."
24. "Wholesale already pays me garbage. This doesn't raise my price."
25. "I make more at the farmers' market on Saturday."
26. "Locking a price in June means I lose if the market spikes in August."

### D. Existing relationships & contracts (the incumbent)
27. "I already sell everything I grow. I don't need buyers."
28. "I've sold to the same three restaurants for ten years. We don't need an app."
29. "I'm under contract with a distributor — I can't sell around them."
30. "My CSA takes most of my volume already."
31. "I sell to a co-op/aggregator; they handle all this."
32. "My handshake deals work fine. Why fix what isn't broken?"
33. "The chef and I just text. It's easier than your app."

### E. Risk & commitment (the fear — the structural core)
34. "I can't promise 40 lbs a week in writing. What if it hails?"
35. "A contract means I get sued when my crop fails."
36. "If I commit to you and a better buyer shows up, I'm stuck."
37. "What if the buyer cancels mid-season — am I on the hook?"
38. "I don't sign things without my [spouse/lawyer] reading them."
39. "Committing a whole season scares me. I plant and pray."

### F. Production fit (irregularity, seasonality, scale)
40. "My yields are too unpredictable to commit to a number."
41. "I only grow 10 weeks a year — what do I do the other 42?"
42. "I'm too small; I can't fill a recurring weekly order."
43. "I'm too big; I move truckloads, not 40-lb cases to restaurants."
44. "I grow 30 different things in tiny amounts — doesn't fit 'one crop, weekly.'"
45. "Restaurants want perfect, uniform produce. Mine's ugly and real."

### G. Logistics & operations
46. "I can't drive into the city for one restaurant — gas eats the profit."
47. "Who handles it if produce spoils in transit? Me?"
48. "I don't have cold storage to hold for a scheduled drop."
49. "My packing is by feel, not 'cases' and 'grades' your form wants."

### H. Structural / philosophical (the hard no)
50. "I farm because I hate being told what to produce. A contract is the opposite of why I do this."

## How many already have contracts?

No clean national stat, so here's the honest shape (US small/mid local farms):

| Bucket | Rough share | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Formal **written** contracts | ~10–20% | Distributors, institutions (schools/hospitals), food hubs |
| Standing **informal** commitments (handshake/text) | ~50–70% | **The real incumbent.** Objections #27–33 |
| CSA volume (spoken for) | ~40% of direct-market farms run one | Objection #30 |
| Truly open, unsold surplus | Smaller and lumpier than you'd hope | See next section |

**You are rarely selling to a farm with zero buyers.** You're selling _"replace the anxiety of handshakes with structure that still flexes"_ — to someone busy, app-skeptical, and previously burned.

## "But don't these farms have crop waste they can't sell?"

Yes — and this is the most important nuance in the whole pitch.

On-farm loss is real and large: depending on crop and year, **~10–20%+ of what's grown is left unharvested, culled for looks, or dumped in a glut.** Market day ends, the truck's still half full, and that kale is worth nothing by Tuesday. The pain is visceral and immediate.

**But here's the trap:** surplus is a **spot** problem, and CropConnect is deliberately **not** a spot marketplace.

- You can't put a _forward contract_ on "the stuff that won't sell," because nobody knows in February what won't sell in August.
- A "dump my surplus cheap" feature would race prices to the bottom — the exact opposite of the _predictability + provability_ we charge for.

So the right way to use waste in the pitch is **cause, not symptom**:

> Surplus is the _symptom_ of having no committed demand. You over-plant and pray because nothing's locked in. A contract attacks the _cause_ — you plant **to** demand you already have, so the waste never gets created in the first place.

That reframes waste from "let me help you sell garbage" (low-status, low-margin) to **"let me help you stop growing garbage you can't sell"** (planning, dignity, margin).

**Optional GTM bridge (not the core):** a lightweight "surplus → quick offer" hook can be a _top-of-funnel_ on-ramp — get a farmer their first easy win on real surplus, then convert: _"you've sold them 3 times now — want to lock it in for the season?"_ Spot is the bait; the contract is the hook. Build it only if first-contact conversion needs the help, and keep it visibly separate from the committed-supply core so it doesn't cannibalize the positioning.

## The real question: why contracts when handshakes work fine?

Be honest with yourself: **for one stable, existing handshake with a chef you've known ten years, a contract adds almost nothing.** If you pitch "replace your handshake," you lose — you're adding friction to something that already works.

The incentive only appears where the handshake is **bad at the job.** Sell those jobs:

### For the farm
1. **Plan production before the season.** A handshake in your head doesn't tell you what to plant in February. A committed contract does. This is the single biggest unlock — and it's the one that actually reduces waste (above).
2. **Scale past a few relationships.** You can hold 3 chefs' verbal terms in your head. You cannot hold 15. Handshakes don't scale; structure does.
3. **A track record you own and can take with you.** Reliability lives on your profile, not trapped in one chef's memory. It wins the _next_ buyer. (This is also CropConnect's anti-leakage moat.)
4. **A financeable asset.** A written, tracked, recurring contract can back a working-capital advance. A handshake can't be borrowed against. (The long-game moat.)
5. **Set-it-once, not re-litigate-weekly.** Handshakes get renegotiated every delivery ("how much you got, what price?"). A contract sets terms once and renews.
6. **Protection when it goes sideways.** Chef leaves, buyer ghosts, quality dispute — you have terms and a record instead of a he-said-she-said.

### For the buyer
1. **Supply security.** Handshake supply evaporates when the farmer gets a better offer or has a bad week. A contract — even a flexible one — puts you first.
2. **Menu & labor planning.** Committed inputs → plan the menu and staff the line with confidence.
3. **Provable provenance.** A documented relationship is a farm-to-table story you can _market_ (QR, farm profile), not just claim.
4. **End the weekly scramble.** Standing terms beat texting five farms every Monday.

### The one-line positioning
> The handshake stays for the chef you've known forever. **CropConnect is for the _next_ ten buyers, for _planning_ what to grow, and for _proving_ you're reliable to people who don't know you yet.**

Handshakes are a great _retention_ tool and a terrible _growth, planning, and trust-building_ tool. Sell growth, planning, and trust.

## What our product already answers (objection → feature)

| Objection cluster | Built-in answer |
|---|---|
| #34–#39 commitment fear | **Quantity bands + crop-failure clause + opt-out window + minimum cycles** |
| #11–#15 trust / will-you-pay | **Reliability score** (real fulfillment history, visible before you commit) |
| #12, #36 "try before I commit" | **Sample shipment stage** — one delivery to approve before locking in |
| #46–#48 logistics / "I can't drive to the city" | **CropConnect courier** (Uber Direct seam) — farm never leaves the field |
| #1–#4 time / tech aversion | **Comfort Mode** + concierge onboarding ("I'll set it up for you") |
| #26 price-lock regret | bands + renegotiable renewal; price is a starting point, not a cage |

## The 5 that actually kill the deal
- **#34/#35 crop-failure fear** → answered (clause + bands). Lead with it.
- **#29 locked to a distributor** → genuinely structural. Disqualify, move on.
- **#27/#28 "I already sell everything"** → not a no, a "why switch?" Needs the 10x hook (planning, reliability, courier).
- **#1/#2 time + tech aversion** → most underrated barrier. Concierge + Comfort Mode is the unlock.
- **#11–#15 no proof / will you exist** → only solved by named local references. Concierge the first 10 so you _have_ them.
