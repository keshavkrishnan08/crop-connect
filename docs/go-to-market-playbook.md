# CropConnect — Go-to-Market Playbook

_How to pitch in person, grow it, hire, and (eventually) run ads. June 2026._

**The one principle:** for the first year this is a **sales + concierge** business, not a growth-hacking/ads business. The product is the enabler; distribution is feet, relationships, density, and renewals. Two-sided local marketplaces die from low liquidity, not bad features — so everything below is about manufacturing liquidity in **one** place.

---

## 1. The in-person pitch

### Before you open your mouth
- **Talk to the decision-maker.** The farm owner, the chef/owner — not the line cook, not the farmhand.
- **Time it.** Restaurants: walk in 2–4pm, between services. Farms: at the market stall, or call after dark when they're off the field.
- **Open with their pain, not your product.** You're a problem-remover, not an app salesman.
- **Have the live demo open** on your phone/laptop (the landing board) — let them touch it. People believe what they can poke.

### Farmer script (~45 seconds)
> "Quick question — you ever over-plant and watch good produce rot because a buyer flaked or the market was slow?
> That's the thing we kill. CropConnect locks in a buyer **before** you plant. And it's flexible — you commit a *range*, never an exact number, and if weather wrecks a week, it's forgiven, no penalty.
> The buyer pays into escrow, so you get paid **the day you deliver** — no chasing checks. And you never leave the farm; we send a courier.
> Want me to set one up with [a specific local restaurant] this week? I'll do all the paperwork — you just say yes to the terms."

### Buyer / chef script (~45 seconds)
> "How many hours a week do you burn texting five farms hoping someone has what you need?
> CropConnect gives you a **standing local supply contract**. You see the farm's reliability score before you commit, you get a **sample shipment first** so you taste it before locking in, and if your menu changes you can opt out with notice.
> Plus the whole farm-to-table story is documented — so you can actually market it.
> Want me to line up a local farm for [their signature ingredient]? I'll handle the match."

### The close that works pre-liquidity
Don't ask them to "sign up." Ask: **"Let me set up one contract for you — free, I'll do the work."** You are the liquidity. The app is your system of record. Get to *yes on one deal*, not *yes on an account*.

### Handle the top 3 objections on the spot
- **"I can't promise a number / what if it hails"** → "You don't. You promise a *range*, and weather shortfalls are forgiven. Watch —" (show the band + crop-failure clause).
- **"I already have my buyers / handshake works"** → "Keep them. This isn't to replace your guy — it's to handle the drop-off, the paperwork, and to guarantee you get paid. Bring your existing buyer on; takes 2 minutes."
- **"Is the money real?"** → "Yes — escrow. Buyer funds it up front, it releases to you the second the delivery's confirmed. Not a handshake, real money." (show the payments panel).

## 2. How to grow it

### Sequence — do NOT skip steps
1. **Concierge (no scale).** Hand-broker **5–10 committed deals** in ONE metro yourself. Match them, write the terms, babysit the first deliveries. Prove they sign **and fulfill and renew** before spending a dollar on growth.
2. **Thin software.** Use the app as the record + workflow; you still match by hand. Get **density** — one restaurant corridor, one farmers-market cluster, one neighborhood.
3. **Density → word of mouth.** Tight food communities talk. 20 connected farms + restaurants in one zip beats 200 scattered nationwide. Chefs refer chefs; farmers refer farmers.
4. **Expand metro by metro**, never thin-and-wide. Each new metro is its own cold start with its own local face.

### The only metric that matters
**Renewals.** A signup is nothing. A first contract can be a favor to you. A *renewed* contract is proof the thing works. Track: contracts signed → fulfilled → **renewed**, and time-to-first-contract.

### Where to physically find them (your real "channels")
- **Supply (farms):** farmers markets (walk the stalls), food hubs, CSAs, co-ops, county ag commissioner / extension offices, regional farm associations.
- **Demand (buyers):** independent restaurants (back door, 2–4pm), small restaurant groups, boutique grocers, bakeries, hotel kitchens, local chef Slack/Facebook groups and associations.
- **Anchor demand first.** Buyers are the constraint — they have distributors. Lock a few hungry buyers, and farms will commit gladly.

## 3. Hiring salespeople

### Founder-led sales first — non-negotiable
You cannot outsource learning the pitch. **Close 15–25 deals yourself.** Out of that you'll have: a tight script, the 5 objections that actually come up, which features close (escrow? courier? crop-failure clause?), and which persona converts. Only then is there a "job" to hire for.

### Your first hire is NOT a slick B2B closer
It's a **market launcher** — someone rooted in local food: an ex-chef, an ex-farmers-market manager, an ex-broadline-distributor rep, a food-co-op organizer. **Relationships and credibility beat sales polish.** A farmer trusts someone who's had dirt under their nails, not a SaaS bro.

### Comp aligned to the real metric
Base + bonus **per renewed contract** (or per active recurring GMV), **not** per signup. Pay for the outcome you actually need, or you'll get a pile of dead accounts.

### Scale model
- One launcher per metro when you expand. Each new market needs a local face and 4–8 weeks of concierge before it self-sustains.
- Only hire after you have a **written playbook**: the scripts above, the objection sheet, a 1-week rep onboarding, and a target list. If you can't hand a new rep a doc that gets them to first close in 2 weeks, you're not ready to hire.

## 4. Ads

### Early: mostly don't
Paid ads into a cold, two-sided, local marketplace are a money incinerator. You pay for clicks → they hit a thin/empty marketplace → bounce. (Our own 100-user simulation showed exactly this churn.) Spend the equivalent on **gas and coffee meeting people**, not on CPMs.

### Do this instead of ads (year one)
- **Feet on the street** (Section 2 channels).
- **Warm intros + referral loops.** Your users already have handshake relationships — incentivize them to **bring their counterpart** onto the platform (they do the trust-transfer for you).
- **Earned media.** Local food press, restaurant newsletters, "meet your farmer" stories. A founder story in a local food blog out-converts any ad.
- **Show the live demo.** The landing's interactive board *is* your best ad — link it everywhere; it sells itself with zero spend.

### When ads finally make sense (after a metro has liquidity)
- **Hyper-local + demand-side.** Geo-fence the metro; target restaurant owners/buyers, not the whole world.
- **Retarget** site/demo visitors who didn't sign up — they already showed intent.
- **Channels:** Instagram for the farm-to-table story; LinkedIn for restaurant groups and procurement; local Google search for "wholesale local produce [city]."
- **Discipline:** CAC must stay below (your fee on a renewed contract × expected lifetime). If a customer's lifetime value can't pay back the ad, the channel is wrong — go back to feet.

---

## TL;DR
- **Pitch:** remove pain, not "use my app." Lead with committed demand (farms) / reliable supply + provenance (buyers). Close on *one concierge deal*, not an account. Show the demo.
- **Grow:** concierge → density in one metro → renewals → expand metro by metro. Renewal is the only real metric.
- **Hire:** founder sells first; first hire is a food-world *market launcher*, paid per renewal, only after a written playbook exists.
- **Ads:** not yet. Feet, warm intros, referral loops, and the live demo. Ads come after liquidity, and only hyper-local + demand-side.
