Done-for-you local sourcing for independent restaurants, run by an AI agent · Indianapolis

A restaurant names one ingredient. Our agent finds the local farms, signs the contract, runs weekly delivery, holds payment in escrow, and tracks the margin. The owner does two things: ask, and put it on the menu. We make money on a monthly service fee, not on the food, so we earn software margins on a problem everyone else tried to solve with trucks.

1 · The problem, in numbers

Independent full-service restaurants run on a net margin of about 3–5% of revenue.1 Food and labor alone consume roughly 60 cents of every dollar.1 There is almost no room to cut, so growth has to come from charging more for the same seats.

Local sourcing is how kitchens do exactly that. 38% of U.S. diners say they are more likely to choose a restaurant that offers locally sourced food,2 and 55% want to know the story behind what they eat.3 But sourcing locally by hand is a second full-time job: finding farms, vetting them, negotiating prices, writing agreements, scheduling deliveries, and covering shortfalls when a crop comes up short. An owner already working 60-hour weeks skips it. The demand exists; the labor to capture it does not.

2 · What we do, step by step

CropConnect is an AI agent that runs local sourcing end to end. The restaurant enters one need — say, 40 lbs of heirloom tomatoes a week. With no further work from the owner, the agent:

  • ranks vetted local farms by crop, distance, reliability, and price;
  • splits the order across several farms if one cannot cover the volume;
  • drafts a supply contract and runs a short negotiation over quality terms (organic, grade, freshness window);
  • schedules weekly deliveries through third-party logistics;
  • holds the food payment in escrow and releases it to the farm only after delivery is confirmed; and
  • tracks the added margin on each dish.

Setup takes about ten minutes, and the first ingredient is free. The product is built and live today, including the agent, the contracts, the escrow, and a free instant sourcing audit any restaurant can run before signing up.

3 · Why now

Two curves crossed. Diner demand for local has risen for years — the share of shoppers who rate ingredient transparency as important went from 69% in 2018 to 76% in 2023.3 And AI agents have only just made the coordination behind local sourcing cheap enough to give away as a done-for-you service. Work that used to require a salaried sourcing manager can now be run by software. This is the first moment both are true at once.

4 · The market

U.S. restaurants did about $1.1 trillion in sales across roughly 1 million locations in 2024.4 Our wedge is the independent full-service segment — the operators who both want local sourcing and lack the staff to do it. We win one metro at a time and earn density before expanding.

In the Indianapolis metro there are roughly 4,500 restaurants; we estimate about 1,500 are target independents.5 At a blended $10,700 per restaurant per year, that is about $16 million of annual revenue available in Indianapolis alone — before a second market.5

5 · Why no one has won this — and exactly why they failed

This is a graveyard, and the cause of death is consistent.

  • Webvan raised more than $800 million to deliver fresh groceries, built its own warehouses and truck fleet, expanded city by city, and filed for bankruptcy in 2001.6
  • Good Eggs raised roughly $50 million to deliver local food, scaled to four cities, and in 2015 laid off most of its staff and retreated to a single market.6
  • Farmigo (2016), Door to Door Organics (2016), and Relay Foods (2016) each tried to aggregate small farms and deliver the food themselves. Each shut the business down.6

The reason was the same every time: they owned the perishable inventory and the logistics. Warehouses, trucks, and spoilage are cash-intensive and low-margin, and the math gets worse — not better — as you add cities. They also sold to consumers, the most price-sensitive buyer, and they expanded geography before the unit economics worked. Meanwhile the large distributors (Sysco, US Foods) serve scale but not small local farms: for them, "local" is a marketing checkbox, not the product.

Why we are structurally different, point by point:

  • We own no warehouses, no trucks, no inventory. The restaurant funds the food at the farm's price — we mark up nothing — and delivery is contracted to third parties at cost.
  • We sell to restaurants on a recurring fee, not to price-sensitive consumers.
  • We earn density in one metro before opening a second, instead of expanding into losses.
  • The coordination that made this business unprofitable by hand is now run by an AI agent. We kept the one part with software economics and left the cash-intensive parts to the people already good at them.

6 · How we make money (so there is no confusion)

Three explicit parts:

  • Food: billed to the restaurant at the farm's price. We make $0 on the food itself.
  • Delivery: passed through at third-party cost. We make $0 on logistics.
  • Service fee: our only revenue. A $299 monthly base plus a per-item fee that scales with volume, blending to about $896 per restaurant per month (~$10,700/year).

Because our costs are software, agent compute, and a thin coordination layer, the service-fee gross margin runs about 80% at scale. Acquisition is led by a part-time industry insider and warm introductions; at ~$896/month and ~80% gross margin, a customer contributes about $717/month, so even a $1,000 acquisition cost pays back in under two months (illustrative).

7 · Why we keep winning (the moat)

  • Two-sided density: once we vet and contract a city's farms, a new entrant has to rebuild the whole local supply base. More restaurants pull more farm capacity; more farms mean better fill.
  • Proprietary data: every contract, delivery, and price trains the agent on local supply, reliability, and demand, so our matching and forecasting beat any generic tool.
  • Switching costs: once we run a kitchen's sourcing, contracts, deliveries, and margins, replacing us means rebuilding their supply chain by hand.
